Design Microscale and Nanoscale Reactors

 Several fundamental design concepts for micron-level chemical laboratories and nanoscale devices have been formulated and tested at the University of Notre Dame. These include electrokinetic and pressure-driven transport mechanisms for single-phase fluids and multi-phase fluids, channel profiling to minimize dispersion of sample slugs, microscale mixing by asymmetric wall double-layers and electrolyte concentration gradients, fabrication of regular nanoscale templates by a new electrochemical pattern formation phenomenon , etching of regimented nanoscale pores from the templates and electrophoretic packing of nanoscale gold colloids into the pores to form miniature packed beds. Known and postulated applications of these devices and designs will be discussed.

Electrokinetically driven flow is a viable means of transporting of electrolyte solutions in microlaboratories. Chemical analysis labs, for example, are already produced by Caliper Technologies. However, the efficiency of such devices is still limited by many transport issues involving dispersion and mixing. Because the flow is produced by net ion migration within thin double layers (about 100 nanometer) at the channel wall, the electokinetic velocity is independent of the streamlines in the bulk. As such, sample slugs tend to be stretched on the inside track of a turn where the transit time is shorter. With finite cross-streamline diffusion, this stretching leads to an effective streamwise Taylor-Aris dispersion that overlap the samples. Since an optimally designed chip would require curved channels due to space considerations, this dispersion problem must be overcome to permit a large number of short sample slugs. We have developed a simulation package for the electric and velocity fields in the channels and an analytical approach , using a Hodograph transformation to conformally map the channel into a strip, to accurately estimate the dispersion of any channel. A variational formulation also allows us to design the optimal channel profiles to minimize dispersion. By minimizing total curvatures and by reducing the streamwise potential gradient and electrokinetic velocity on the inside track, a unique profile can be produced for a given flow rate, potential drop and channel length. These optimized profiles are then tested with macroscopic prototypes.

 For microreactors, mixing of reactants becomes a fundamental problem as high Reynolds number turbulent or inertial mixing at usual scales do not exist. To compound the problem, the lack of velocity gradient across streamlines in electrokinetic flow minimizes dispersion due to convection-diffusion coupling. We propose two new mixing mechanisms for viscous flow at micron scales. By applying an electric field or chemical additive to one side of the channel, we shall attempt to produce a cross-stream velocity gradient due to the asymmetric double layer across the channel. Another mechanism is to change the electrolyte concentrations of two merging streams such that gradients in the conductivity exists in both the streamwise and normal directions. These gradients produce normal flows and counter flows that combine to produce a mixing vortex in the channel. Experiments with macroscopic channels indicate the quality of mixing (the mixing time) can be improved by three orders of magnitude with this design.

 Extending electrokinetic flow to multi-phase flow can potentially apply the same technology to non-electrolyte fluids. A non-wetting organic sample, for example, can be transported by an electrolyte fluid around it. Air bubbles and liquid slugs can also act as driving pistons and sample spacers. We find, however, that multi-fluid electrokinteic flow must be designed with additional considerations. Since the electrolyte film around the bubble or drop is smaller in cross-section area than the full channel area behind it, the electric field, resistance and electrokinetic velocity all increase proportionally within the film. However, the total flow rate remains the same due to this scaling. As such, the electrolyte film simply transports around the stationary bubble/drop. To induce the latter to move, we introduce surfactants to endow the interface with traction and to introduce asymmetric double layers across the film. With this technique, we can induce bubble/drop motion with a speed that exceeds single-phase electrolyte electrokinetic velocity. However, surfactant double layers tend to be very thick such that they can completely suppress the current necessary for electrokinetic flow at high bubble velocities when the film is thin. There is hence a window of surfactant and electrolyte concentrations when such multi-phase electrokinetic transport is possible. We estimate this window theoretically by analyzing the intefacial problem,with capillary, viscous, electrokinetic and Marangoni effects, via matched asymptotics. The results are favorable compared to our experiments.

In an earlier study, we have discovered that double layer distortion can produce patterns on a electrode dissolving in an electropolishing solution [Phys Rev B, 56. 2818(1997)]. Our solution of the Poisson equation for the electric potential within the double layer indicates that the potential gradient is higher at the crests of the interface and lower at the troughs. Hence, polar or polarizable molecules in the electrolyte pack preferentially at the crests and reduce their dissolution rate. This instability is balanced by surface diffusion to produce patterns with length scale in the 100 nm range. Under specific conditions specified by our theory, perfectly regimented hexagonal hills can be be produced on an aluminum electrode at that length scale. If we then anodize the electrode, anodization pits around 10 nm in diameter then grow at the troughs of the electropolishing patterns. The preconditioned template then produces a very regimented hexagonal arrays of nanoscale pits over a large (cmxcm) surface. These tailor-designed pits can be used as membranes or as etching masks to make regimented pits on other materials. We have successfully done so on silicone.

 A miniature packed bed results when we electrophoretically pack nanoscale gold particles, coated with ligands, into the above pit array in silicone. Such gold particles are found to aggregate on alumina surfaces and seem to be stabilized within the pits. Packing at this scale increases not only the surface area but may also change the catalytic activity of the bio and chemical catalysts. Other applications of this nanoscale device will be discussed.

These studies contain some of the most important physics and chemistry issues that must be understood before microlaboratories and nanoscale devices can become commercially viable.
