A Novel Catalytic Converter Re-design for Rapid Ignition

Presented at AIChE National Conference, Dallas, TX, 11/99
In Preparation for submission to AIChE Journal

The catalytic converter used today is cold and ineffective during the first three minutes after a car is started. A design will be presented here which shortens this transient period during cold starts and hence drastically reduces pollution emissions. Because the current monolith is designed to maximize interphase heat transfer, there is very little thermal dispersion. As a result, a thermal front remains sharp as it travels down the monolith and ignition occurs at this front near the exit of the converter. This down-stream ignition position renders the ignition sensitive to flow rate fluctuations that may push the ignition zone out of the converter and extinguish it. In contrast, if the interphase heat transfer is diminished, a large Taylor-Aris thermal dispersion arises, and a much more robust leading-edge ignition will take place in the converter. This preferred ignition position also allows convection to light off the remaining monolith whereas downstream ignition can only do so through the slow process of upstream thermal diffusion.

The earlier analytical theory of Leighton and Chang (AIChE J., vol. 41, p. 1898 (1995)) for a step thermal front explicitly and qualitatively delineates these two different ignition behaviors with a single dimensionless parameter that is a ratio between the square of the axial convection and axial dispersion length scales during the homogeneous time for ignition. Numerical simulations confirm this desirable leading-edge ignition under realistic transient driving conditions, and an effective re-design of the current catalytic converter system is proposed. To facilitate the design, the theoretical model of Leighton and Chang has been modified to predict the ignition time and location for any catalytic converter design under transient start-up conditions.

The re-design consists of a bypass stream containing an electric pre-heater and a cheap pre-igniter. By adjusting the fraction of gas entering the bypass and using a very low wattage power supply, the electric pre-heater is able to raise the gas temperature beyond a precise minimum determined by the analytical theory, such that the exiting hot gas subsequently triggers a robust and rapid leading-edge ignition in the pre-igniter. The heat released from CO oxidation in the pre-igniter then rapidly raises the temperature in the original converter, which is not removed. When the main converter is warm enough, the bypass through the pre-igniter is eliminated and the main converter lights off. Despite the added complexity, the bypass has two distinct advantages: prevention of poisoning at the pre-igniter, and reduction of the required power input to the electric pre-heater to the extent that an extra power supply is not required.

The analytical theory is used to optimize the key design parameter, the power supply to the electric pre-heater. The optimal design is shown to reduce the light-off time to 12 seconds, reducing pollution of CO and NO by over 90%, and reducing non-methane hydrocarbon pollution by almost 100% over the current converter when it is equipped with an optional hydrocarbon trap. Verification of the optimal design, which easily beats ULEV (Ultra-Low Emission Vehicle) standards while requiring only a 1200 W power supply and 4 W-h of energy, is obtained through numerical simulation of the Federal Test Procedure (FTP) driving test and various transient emission histories. Experiments will also be carried out on a Honda Civic to verify the validity of the new design.











































This figure shows numerical simulation results for the catalytic converter with a feed temperature of 600 K and with 2% CO in the exhaust. Each line corresponds to a snapshot of solid phase temperatures in the catalytic converter taken 5 seconds apart. It is clear to see that the converter ignites at the converter exit in a time of about 40 seconds. This downstream ignition, which occurs when the gas-solid thermal equilibration time is much larger than the reaction timescale, has two disadvantages.

The first is that the ignition heat is wasted, as for some pollutants to be removed the entire converter must be ignited. It takes an additional 3-5 minutes for the process of upstream thermal diffusion to lightoff the rest of the converter. During this time, there may be high pollution levels. Secondly, the downstream ignition is believed to be unstable to fluctuations in mass flowrate through the catalytic converter. An increase in the flowrate may push the ignited zone beyond the converter exit, such that an ignition does not take place and pollution is high.

This is the predominant ignition mechanism in aged catalytic converters during city driving conditions. It occurs because over the course of the ignition time, the gas and solid phases reach thermal equilibrium, and the thermal front is not smoothed much near the point at which reaction takes place. Consequently, the front passes through most of the converter before the ignition takes place.













































This figure shows numerical simulation results for the catalytic converter with a feed temperature of 700 K and with 2% CO in the exhaust. Each line corresponds to a snapshot of solid phase temperature in the catalytic converter taken 3 seconds apart. It is clear to see that the catalytic converter ignites at the entrance of the converter in less than 10 seconds. This leading-edge ignition, which occurs when the gas-solid thermal equilibration time is on the same order as the reaction time, is believed to have two advantages over the downstream ignition.

The first is that the ignition heat is used. Gas convection quickly lights off the rest of the converter in less than 30 seconds. The second advantage of leading-edge ignition is that it is stable to fluctuations in flowrate. No matter what the gas flowrate is, the ignition will always occur at the leading edge of the converter.

This is the predominant ignition mechanism in aged catalytic converters during highway driving conditions. It occurs because over the course of the ignition time, the gas and solid phases do not reach thermal equilibrium, and the thermal front is smoothed greatly near the point at which reaction takes place. Consequently, the ignition occurs far upstream of the front at the leading edge of the converter.

This plot shows the scaled ignition time as a function of the parameter X, which is a ratio of the gas-solid equilibration time to the reaction time. At high temperatures, the reaction time is equal to the gas-solid equilibration time (such that X = 3.0 when T = 750 K). Theoretical predictions of the ignition time compare well with the simulation results, where the feed temperature and concentration are kept constant, but the gas flowrate is allowed to fluctate at various periods and fluctuation amplitudes. At low temperatures, the gas-solid equilibration time is larger than the reaction time (such that X = 0.05 when T = 550 K), and theoretical predictions of the ignition time do not agree with simulation results. In fact, a point is reached (about 560 K) where the converter never ignites! As the maximum temperature exiting the engine during city driving conditions is around this value, there is a potential of no ignition at all.
























To counter the lack of ignition during city driving, the Notre Dame catalytic converter was developed. It consists of a bypass channel which contains an electric heater and a pre-converter. The flow through the dual butterfly valve is controlled with two thermocouples. The flow through the bypass is adjusted so that the maximum temperature of the gas exiting the heater is high enough to lead to a stable, rapid, leading-edge ignition in the pre-converter. The key feature of this design is the fact that as the flowrate through the heater is reduced, the temperature rise exiting it increases.

Once the pre-converter ignites, the valve is ajdusted so that all of the gas flows through the bypass. This allows the rest of the exhaust system to heat up rapidly. Once the main converter is warm enough, the valve is adjusted again so that none of the gas enters the bypass. Since the bypass is only used during cold starts, the key units in the bypass will be preserved and can have long lifetimes without replacement.

This figure shows some experimental results. The car shown above was driven to mimic city driving. It remained in place during a test but the engine was revved with transients of one minute duration at set rpm levels followed by three minute idling phases.

Shown here is a plot of relative lightoff time (lightoff time with new design / lightoff time of old design) as a function of the bypass angle. As the angle is increased, less gas is allowed to pass through the electric heater, which leads to higher temperature feeds to the pre-converter. A smaller lightoff time is expected, and is seen here for bypass angles up to 75 degrees. For angles beyond that, there is excessive heat loss and the ignition time increases.

The results seen here indicate a reduction in the ignition time from 50 - 70%, which is a good improvement over the standard design.
