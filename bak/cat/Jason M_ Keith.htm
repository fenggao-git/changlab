Research Projects

  • Design of Fast-Igniting Catalytic Converter Systems


    • Numerical results have shown that older catalytic converters may never ignite during city driving conditions, which may explain high pollution levels in congested areas such as Los Angeles or Houston. However, further results indicate that the same converters ignite rapidly when vehicles are at highway speeds. This is due to the presence of a large Taylor-Aris thermal dispersion mechanism which smooths a hot thermal front as it enters the cold converter during vehicle start-up, such that the ignition occurs at the leading edge of the converter. This mechanism does not exist during city driving as the gas and solid equilibrate rapidly such that near the thermal front, there is no smoothing. This results in a downstream ignition which is proportional to the mass flowrate through the exhaust. For typical city driving, fluctuations actually extinguish any potential ignition.

    These fundamental concepts in fluid mechanics and reaction engineering are utilized here to design a new catalytic converter system. The new design, which has an added bypass channel, an electrically heated converter, and a pre-converter has been shown in an experimental prototype to reduce the ignition time by over a factor of two compared with the current converter. Numerical simulations of an optimized system indicate a potential for a more dramatic pollution reduction.

  • Design of Thermally Stable Reverse-Flow Reactors


    • In the chemical industry, fixed bed reactors have been utilized more frequently for pollutant removal due to inexpensive operating costs. However, extended lean periods where there is no fuel may extinguish the bed. Consequently, the flow of gas through the bed is reversed periodically to maintain a bed in an ignited state. These reactors have not been used much in industry, however, because of their potential to exhibit thermal runaway as very sharp temperature spikes form in the bed during rich feed conditions.

    The fixed bed reactor is redesigned here by placing cylindrical metal rods in an ordered pattern within the bed. This allows for a transient Taylor-Aris thermal dispersivity to develop which smooths the energy in the temperature spikes throughout the bed. A theoretical model has been developed to predict the performance of such high-dispersion reverse-flow reactors during rich and lean conditions. Design criteria have also been developed which determine the optimal metal rod size, spacing, and reversal time for any chemical reaction. The resulting bed can operate over twenty times longer than their current industrial counterparts.
