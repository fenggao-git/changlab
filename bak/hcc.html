Hsueh-Chia Chang
Bayer Professor of Engineering

B.S., California Institute of Technology, 1976
Ph.D., Princeton University, 1980
 

Areas of Research:           Complex Fluids and Flows
                                            Synthesis of Novel Materials
                                            Transport Phenomena
                                             Reaction Engineering
 
 

    Professor Chang is interested in spatial and temporal dynamics in hydrodynamics, reaction-diffusion systems, surfactant aggregation, molecular dynamics, thermal ignition and electrochemical dissolution. Current projects include wave dynamics on falling films, mass/heat transfer and reaction enhancement due to chaotic and turbulent mixing, contact-line dynamics, soliton bifurcations, molecular diffusion in zeolites, molecular lubricant design, nanofabrication by electrodissolution instabilities, flow transition in multi-phase channel flow, modification of classical transition-state reaction and transport theory using Hamiltonian dynamics, ignition of catalytic converters and stability of reverse-flow reactors.

    His approach is analytical in most cases with some supporting numerical and experimental components. Perturbation, bifurcation and matched asymptotic analyses are used to decipher the proper scaling laws, the pertinent physics and chemistry and the pattern selection mechanism.

    His group has deciphered the dynamics of wave evolution on a falling film and attributed downstream wave texture coarsening to a unique irreversible wave coalescence phenomenon that is quite distinct from the classical interaction between solitons of conservative systems. He has revealed a dominant mechanism for rivulet formation during film coating involving a transition from rolling to sliding motion. His group has also applied nonlinear wave theory to analyze atomization, slug formation and wave-induced enhanced transports in multi-phase flow systems. Renormalization techniques are used to obtain effective mass transport in turbulent flow and random media.  He has determined that the major stochastic driving force in molecular diffusion through zeolites is low-frequency acoustic lattice vibration and has derived an explicit expression for the infinite-dilution diffusivity. He has examined the effect of two-phase thermal dispersion on reactor stability and ignition of catalytic converters. Another  work promises to decipher the dynamics of pattern formation during metal dissolution in an electrolyte solution.
